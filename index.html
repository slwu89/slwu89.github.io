Research Interests

Major Interests:

  • Mechanistic stochastic modelling, especially non-Markovian or delayed systems
  • Uncertainty quantification for computationally expensive models
  • Graphical methods to specify models (Petri Nets, compartmental model diagrams, DAGs)

Minor Interests:

  • Probabilistic risk assessment for rare events
    I am very interested in thinking about what we can learn from earthquake forecasting here.
  • Epistemic uncertainty in stochastic models
    I haven't thought about it very deeply, but I like Keith Beven's work on this, from hydrology.
  • Variance reduction for Monte Carlo simulation

Introduction

My middle name is Lawrence. I'm an epidemiologist raised by wolves physicists. I attended the University of California (UC), Irvine from 2008 to 2013 where I studied International Studies & Statistics. I recieved my MPH in Epidemiology & Biostatistics from the UC Berkeley in 2016. I am currently a 5th year PhD Candidate in Epidemiology with a designated emphasis in Computational Biology at UC Berkeley. My PhD advisor is John M. Marshall; my applied work at Berkeley involves using stochastic mechanistic models to evaluate potential effects of genetic control strategies upon mosquito-borne disease burden. I draw on a wide range of tools to design models, including dynamical systems and stochastic processes to formulate models, Petri Nets to structure simulation software, and statistical methods to estimate parameters from laboratory data.

I am involved in a long term collaboration with David L. Smith at the Malaria Control and Elimination team housed at the Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation. My work is on structuring large scale models of malaria transmission, at the national scale, or larger. To do so, we have to build reliable, tested software. When we can, we use analytic results (stationary distributions or transition probabilities) to rigorously test our scientific code. We use graphical models, including Petri Nets and directed acyclic graphs to go from a whiteboard description of malaria ecology to a computer program. I also advise on statistical models as needed.

I have been involved in various other research projects, including my current position as a research intern at Microsoft Research where I work on detailed stochastic models of mosquito ecology under Simon D.W. Frost. Previously I have been at the Malaria Elimination Initiative at UC San Francisco, and spent a summer in Sichuan Province, China studying possible genetic biomarkers of susceptibility to schistosomiasis infection at the Berkeley Schistosomiasis Group, under Robert C. Spear. During the first year of my PhD I attended the Complex Systems Summer School (2017) at the Santa Fe Institute and found it life changing.

My father is Cantonese Chinese from Hong Kong and my mother is Scandinavian & Oglala Sioux from San Diego, California. I use he/him/his. I identify as mixed-race (or hapa, but see this). I grew up in San Diego but consider Southern California broadly to be my home. Before coming to Berkeley I worked as a computer hardware repairperson for a cardiology group, office assistant at UC Irvine, waiter at a Taiwanese beef noodle soup restaurant, and medical assistant at a pediatric office. My favorite TV show is Battlestar Galactica. I like to read books; some recent favorites are the Area X trilogy by Jeff VanderMeer and the Three Body Problem by Liu Cixin.

In addition to English I speak fluent Mandarin Chinese. Languages I can speak to computers are R (native), C++ (fluent), C (conversational), and Julia (conversational).
